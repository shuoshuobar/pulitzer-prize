Just announced last week, the 2025 Pulitzer Prize for International Reporting goes to Declan Walsh and the staff of The New York Times for their coverage of Sudan’s conflict. This adds to the three prizes the NYT collected over the past year.

“For their revelatory investigation of the conflict in Sudan, including reporting on foreign influence and the lucrative gold trade fueling it, and chilling forensic accounts of the Sudanese forces responsible for atrocities and famine,” as stated on the Pulitzer Prize webpage.

Each year, a committee at Columbia University’s Pulitzer Prize Board reviews reporting from the past year to recognize the most eminent stories. The prize for international reporting specifically honors journalism crossing borders — geographically, politically and ethically. A look at the awarded pieces reminds people around the world of global epics that have taken place.

As world chaos comes in tandem with each other, the Pulitzer Prize for international reporting often mirrors global crises. Last year, the prize was granted for reporting on Hamas’ October 7 attack and Israel’s military response in Gaza. And a year ago, it went to wartime coverage in Ukraine, with the atrocities committed in Bucha.

At the dawn of the Cold War, Paul W. Ward was awarded for his series Life in the Soviet Union. The year 1948 became the inaugural Pulitzer in international reporting and The Baltimore Sun claimed it. Zooming out to tallies over time, the NYT keeps the highest records in foreign correspondence, followed by other major outlets, like The Washington Post and The Associated Press.

For its wide-ranging and revelatory coverage of Hamas’ lethal attack in southern Israel on October 7, Israel’s intelligence failures and the Israeli military’s sweeping, deadly response in Gaza.

2023

PRIZE RECIPIENT: The New York Times

For their unflinching coverage of Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, including an eight-month investigation into Ukrainian deaths in the town of Bucha and the Russian unit responsible for the killings.

❄️January

Russia’s Invasion of Ukraine (2022)

2022

PRIZE RECIPIENT: The New York Times

For courageous and relentless reporting that exposed the vast civilian toll of U.S.-led airstrikes, challenging official accounts of American military engagements in Iraq, Syria, and Afghanistan.

Yearlong: Pentagon Documents Reveal Hidden Civilian Casualties

“ … a hidden Pentagon archive of the American air war in the Middle East since 2014.” (2014)

🏖️July

Syrian Airstrike Tragedy: Official Accounts Challenged

“ ... American Special Operations forces bombed what they believed were three ISIS ‘staging areas’ on the outskirts of Tokhar, a riverside hamlet in northern Syria. They reported 85 fighters killed. In fact, they hit houses far from the front line, where farmers, their families and other local people sought nighttime sanctuary from bombing and gunfire. More than 120 villagers were killed.” (2016)

🍂November

Ramadi Strike Killed Child, Not Enemy Combatant

“... After observing a man dragging an “unknown heavy object” into an ISIS “defensive fighting position,” American forces struck a building in Ramadi, Iraq. A military review found that the object was actually “a person of small stature” — a child — who died in the strike.” (2015)

2021

PRIZE RECIPIENT: BuzzFeed News

For a series of clear and compelling stories that used satellite imagery and architectural expertise, as well as interviews with two dozen former prisoners, to identify a vast new infrastructure built by the Chinese government for the mass detention of Muslims.

End of the Year: China’s Hidden Detention System

“With at least tens of thousands of detainees crowded into government buildings repurposed as camps by the end of 2017, the government began building the largest new facilities in the spring of 2018. Several were complete by October 2018, with further facilities built through 2019 and construction of a handful more continuing even now.” (2017)

2020

PRIZE RECIPIENT: The New York Times

For a set of enthralling stories, reported at great risk, exposing the predations of Vladimir Putin’s regime.

Yearlong: Putin’s Regime and Its Methods

“In 2006, Russian President Vladimir V. Putin signed a law legalizing targeted killings abroad, and Ukrainian officials say teams of Russian hit men operate freely inside the country.” (2016)

2019

PRIZE RECIPIENT: Associated Press

For a revelatory yearlong series detailing the atrocities of the war in Yemen, including theft of food aid, deployment of child soldiers, and torture of prisoners.

Yearlong: Yemen War — Humanitarian Crisis Continues

“The war in Yemen began in March 2015 after Houthi rebels swept out of the mountains and occupied northern Yemen, forcing the government of Yemeni President Abed Rabbo Mansour Hadi into exile.” (2015)

2019

PRIZE RECIPIENT: Reuters

For expertly exposing the military units and Buddhist villagers responsible for the systematic expulsion and murder of Rohingya Muslims from Myanmar, courageous coverage that landed its reporters in prison.

🍂September

Evidence of Mass Killings in Rakhine State

On Sept. 2, Buddhist villagers and Myanmar troops killed 10 Rohingya men in Myanmar’s restive Rakhine state. (2018)

2018

PRIZE RECIPIENT: Reuters

For relentless reporting that exposed the brutal killing campaign behind Philippines President Rodrigo Duterte’s war on drugs.

🏖️July

Duterte’s Drug War Leaves Rising Death Toll

The data also shows a sharp increase in the number of drug suspects declared dead on arrival in these two districts each month. There were 10 cases at the start of the drug war in July 2016, representing 13 percent of police drug shooting deaths. By January 2017, the tally had risen to 51 cases or 85 percent. The totals grew along with international and domestic condemnation of Duterte’s campaign. (2016)

2017

PRIZE RECIPIENT: The New York Times

For agenda-setting reporting on Vladimir Putin’s efforts to project Russia’s power abroad, revealing techniques that included assassination, online harassment and the planting of incriminating evidence on opponents.

🌿May

The Return of Political Poisonings

“While Mr. Kara-Murza survived, few others in his position have proved as lucky. He said he was certain he had been the target of a security service poisoning. Used extensively in the Soviet era, political murders are again playing a prominent role in the Kremlin’s foreign policy, the most brutal instrument in an expanding repertoire of intimidation tactics intended to silence or otherwise intimidate critics at home and abroad.” (2015)

2016

PRIZE RECIPIENT: The New York Times

For thoroughly reported and movingly written accounts giving voice to Afghan women who were forced to endure unspeakable cruelties.

Yearlong: Afghan Women Under Threat

“Parveena’s story — she was one of six policewomen killed in 2013 — is an extreme case, but it reflects the dangers and difficulties of Afghan policewomen and the broader Western effort to engineer gender equality in Afghanistan. The plight of women under the Taliban captured the Western imagination, and their liberation became a rallying cry. A flood of money and programs poured into Afghanistan, for girls’ schools and women’s shelters and television shows, all aimed at elevating women’s status ...” (2013)

2015

PRIZE RECIPIENT: The New York Times

For courageous front-line reporting and vivid human stories on Ebola in Africa, engaging the public with the scope and details of the outbreak while holding authorities accountable.

❄️December

Ebola Outpaces Aid Response in West Africa

As the Ebola epidemic intensifies across parts of West Africa, nations and aid agencies are pledging to respond with increasing force. But the disease has already raced far ahead of the promises, sweeping into areas that had been largely spared the onslaught and are not in the least prepared for it. (2014)

2014

PRIZE RECIPIENT: Reuters

For their courageous reports on the violent persecution of the Rohingya, a Muslim minority in Myanmar that, in efforts to flee the country, often falls victim to predatory human-trafficking networks.

🌱March

Myanmar’s Democratic Transition Threatened by Ethnic Cleansing

“Ethnic hatred has been unleashed in Myanmar since 49 years of military rule ended in March 2011. And it is spreading, threatening the country’s historic democratic transition. Signs have emerged of ethnic cleansing, and of impunity for those inciting it.” (2011)
